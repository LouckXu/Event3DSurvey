A Survey of 3D Reconstruction with Event Cameras

Chuanzhi Xu, Haoxian Zhou, Langyi Chen, Haodong Chen, Zeke Zexi Hu, Zhicheng Lu, Ying Zhou, Vera Chung, Qiang Qu, and Weidong Cai

A comprehensive review of event-driven 3D reconstruction, organized by sensing setup, reconstruction strategy, output representation, datasets, evaluation metrics, and open research challenges.

PDF arXiv BibTeX

Why this survey

Event cameras are changing the reconstruction pipeline

Event cameras asynchronously report per-pixel brightness changes instead of recording full frames at fixed rates. Their microsecond temporal resolution, high dynamic range, low latency, and low redundancy make them well suited to high-speed motion, difficult lighting, and low-power robotics or AR/VR systems.

Survey architecture

A taxonomy built around sensing, processing, and representation

The paper separates event-based 3D reconstruction by camera setup first, then by reconstruction methodology and output type. This makes it easier to compare older geometric pipelines with recent neural rendering systems.

1. Fundamentals

Event camera vs. frame camera, event triggering, event stream representation, and reconstruction output types.

2. Stereo methods

Geometry-based depth recovery, disparity-free optimization, unsupervised embedding, and deep stereo processing.

3. Monocular methods

Motion-prior geometry, learning-based mesh or voxel prediction, event NeRF, and monocular event 3DGS.

4. Multimodal methods

Event streams fused with structured light, RGB-D, blurry RGB, depth, NeRF, and 3D Gaussian Splatting.

5. Datasets and metrics

Public benchmarks, dataset visualization, geometry metrics, IoU/F-score, PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS.

6. Research gaps

Benchmarks, simulation, event representation, dynamic scenes, extreme settings, hardware synchronization, and scalability.

Methods

From stereo depth maps to event-guided neural rendering

The survey tracks the field's movement from explicit event geometry and depth mapping toward high-fidelity NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting.

Stereo event cameras

Depth through asynchronous correspondence and geometric fusion

Stereo systems use synchronized event viewpoints to infer disparity, depth, and point clouds. The survey distinguishes methods that perform explicit disparity matching from approaches that bypass matching through time-surface alignment, DSI fusion, or event ray density.

  • Geometry-based: disparity matching and correspondence-free optimization.
  • Learning-based: self-organizing maps and deep stereo depth networks.
  • Typical outputs: sparse or dense depth maps, point clouds, and voxels.

Monocular event cameras

Lightweight sensing with stronger priors or learned representations

Monocular event cameras remove the stereo baseline, so reconstruction depends on motion, temporal consistency, learned shape priors, or differentiable event rendering. The survey separates traditional 3D outputs from NeRF and 3DGS methods.

  • Traditional outputs: depth maps, meshes, voxels, and point clouds.
  • NeRF: brightness difference and brightness change-rate modelling.
  • 3DGS: image guidance, geometric optimization, and end-to-end learning.

Multimodal event systems

Events as temporal anchors for RGB, RGB-D, depth, and structured light

Multimodal pipelines use events to compensate for motion blur, low light, and fast dynamics while other sensors supply appearance, depth, or absolute scale. Recent work increasingly integrates event streams with NeRF and 3DGS for photorealistic reconstruction.

  • Traditional outputs: structured-light and RGB-D fusion.
  • NeRF: static and dynamic scene reconstruction with blurry RGB and events.
  • 3DGS: static deblurring and dynamic deformable Gaussian models.

Evaluation

Metrics depend on the reconstruction target

Because event-based reconstruction outputs vary from explicit geometry to neural rendering, the survey groups evaluation metrics by output type.

Geometry and depth

Mean error, median error, relative error, RMSE, and MAE for depth maps, point clouds, and meshes.

Voxel or shape recovery

IoU and F-score for dense object reconstruction and volumetric occupancy.

Neural rendering

PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS for novel-view synthesis and perceptual photorealism.

Research gaps

Open problems for event-driven 3D reconstruction

The paper highlights gaps across data, modelling, deployment, and sensor fusion. These directions are especially important as the field moves from isolated demonstrations toward reproducible and scalable systems.

Standard benchmarks

Large-scale real datasets, common acquisition pipelines, and public leaderboards.

Native event simulation

3D simulation that avoids frame-interpolation artifacts and better matches event physics.

Event representation

Unified comparisons, adaptive encodings, and geometry-aware event representations.

Dynamic scenes

Motion-aware NeRF and 3DGS models with real-time structure reconstruction.

Extreme conditions

Evaluation under high speed, low illumination, and high dynamic range scenarios.

Challenging materials

Low-texture, reflective, transparent, non-Lambertian, non-rigid, and deformable targets.

Hardware constraints

Resolution, timestamp precision, bandwidth, calibration, and multimodal synchronization.

Scalability

Efficient architectures for mobile, embedded, and large-scale reconstruction.

New modalities

LiDAR, polarization, IMU, event-based time-of-flight, and selective modality activation.
